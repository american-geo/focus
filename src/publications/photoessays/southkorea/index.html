Schools in South Korea: Where have all the children gone?

Mrs./Mr. Author
Department of Geography, Columbian College of Arts and Sciences, George Washington University
DOI: 10.21690/foge/2018.61.2p

View from a bay-side fishing village on Punghwailju-ro (ro is street), Sanyang-eup (eup is district), Tongyeong-si (si is city), Gyeongsangnam-do (do is province), South Korea.

During the summer of 2017, I was traveling around South Korea as a part of a delegation of U.S. educators whose job was to prepare lesson plans for the new National Atlas of Korea Volumes II and III. I am a high school geography teacher in Tennessee and my main focus for the two-week trip was on getting a better understanding of education and population in South Korea. For the previous National Atlas of Korea Volume I, I developed a lesson plan on South Korea’s aging population and declining birth rate. All three volumes of The National Atlas of Korea, my lesson plan, and three other lesson plans from three other high school geography teachers can be freely downloaded (in public domain) from: http://nationalatlas.ngii.go.kr/pages/page_1.php. Six more lesson plans are currently being developed and shall also be posted on the same website when completed. Volume IV, titled The National Atlas of Korea: Comprehensive Edition, is written specifically for use for secondary education in geography and social studies. It is expected to be published to the same website in Spring 2018. All volumes are written in both Korean and English languages. U.S. and all English-speaking geography and social studies teachers, especially Advanced Placement Human Geography teachers, are encouraged to download the public domain atlases and adopt these lesson plans for use in their classrooms.


During the trip to South Korea, we were able to visit some of the rural areas of the country to see firsthand the impact that a severely declining birth rate and an increasing elderly population can have on such vulnerable communities, and especially on their schools. Figure 1 shows such a rural community where fishing is the main economic activity.
The sad reality is that many of South Korea’s rural communities are dying, or at the very least on life-support. The opportunities for a better living, better jobs, better housing, and more conveniences can be found in more populated urban areas of South Korea. Many young people who grow up in rural areas move to the cities and suburbs seeking these opportunities.
The following excerpt by Choe Sang-Hun in her New York Times article, As South Korean Villages Empty, More Primary Schools Face Closings,” details the reality for many of the small towns and villages across South Korea:

“Villages around here have no more children to send,” the school’s only teacher, Lee Sung-kyun, said recently, looking over an empty, weed-filled playground surrounded by old cherry trees. “Young people have all gone to cities to find work and get married there.”
Most South Koreans now live in the tall apartment buildings that are spread out like dominoes across South Korean cities, but many still bemoan the shrinking of rural communities.

The same can be said for many of the rural communities in Tennessee where I grew up. I did what so many South Koreans did; I moved to a bigger city because of a better job opportunity. Unfortunately for my small town, many of my classmates did the same, and the town’s growth has stagnated. So when we traveled to some of these rural South Korean communities, I could see a lot of similarities with my own hometown in southern middle Tennessee.

A good example of this is that the high school that I attended has, in the last several years, continually dropped in enrollment to the point that it is currently in the small-school classification in the state of Tennessee. The same is happening to many of the schools across South Korea. With their enrollment dropping, their future and the future of the community of which they are a part of are uncertain. This article will provide a snapshot of three different schools in South Korea: 1) A small elementary school in a rural fishing village, 2) a closed elementary school in an inland agricultural community, and 3) a high school in the urban area of Seoul. Together, these paint an interesting picture of the impact of demographic changes that have been occurring throughout South Korea.

Sanyang Elementary School

The first elementary school I want to discuss is the Sanyang Elementary School. A picture of a street sign (Figure 2) that indicated “388 Punghwailju-ro” led me to find and understand the Korean description of an address location.
Figure 2 shows the street address sign of the school and the Google Map location of the school at two different scales.
As with many projects, it was easy to get distracted from the main topic. I didn’t know exactly what Punjhwailju-ro was, and when I finally located the school on Google Maps I was even more confused, or maybe intrigued, in trying to read the address. As someone who spends much of my time teaching and learning geography, I had found a list of places that I wanted—or even needed—to understand.
When I located the school I just didn’t understand how to read the address, but fortunately in the age of the Internet the answer was just a few clicks away. I found the website “South Korea Postal Code” (https://kor.youbianku.com), and it broke down the address so that I could actually make sense of it. The complete locational description of the school is: 388 Punghwailju-ro (ro is street), Sanyang-eup (eup is district), Tongyeong-si (si is city), Gyeongsangnam-do (do is province), South Korea. Now that I understood that South Korean addresses were broken down from street, to district, to city, to province, and finally to country, I understood exactly where the school was located by address. Other administrative subdivisions include gun (county) and three different subdivisions of eup, namely myeon, gu, and dong.

Figure 3 shows the four levels of Korean administrative divisions: do (province), si (city), eup (district), and ro (street) on Google Map.

The most intriguing part of this school is that there are only 9 student and 4 teachers. Yes that is correct: there are only nine students in the entire school: eight boys and one girl. The student-teacher ratio is obviously great! Figure 10 above shows all the member of the school. However, as can be easily imagined, the per student cost of operating and maintaining this school is also very high. But the geographic reality is that rural communities are so far from urban centers that children from remaining rural communities have such great difficulties in every aspect of attending an urban school.

The Vice-Principal walks down the empty hallway.

What is happening in these rural communities and that their schools will almost surely close in the next few years? Karl Schultz explains in his article, “The End of South Korea’s Rural Schools,” about the reality for many of the rural schools in one of which he is a teacher:
The elderly residents of Anpyeong literally grew up with the school, which was founded in 1952. It is the only symbol of youth in a blighted, aging town. For the few student-age residents that remain, having a local school means they can still live at home and do not have to deal with a long daily commute.
A school in each town may have made sense in the 1950s and 1960s, when most of these schools were being built, but they don’t make as much sense today. In 1955, for example, Anpyeong Middle School graduated 117 students (its first graduating class). Today, that number has shrunk to just two students per year.

Part of the immigrant experience is the struggle to maintain connections over great distances. Folkloric dancing is one important way in which children born of Bolivian parents and now living in the United States are exposed to Bolivian culture. In this picture, Bolivia children are participating in a Bolivia festival held each August in northern Virginia. Events such as these draw thousands of immigrants and their children who desire to maintain their connections to a far away home. Another strategy is to have beauty pageants, crowning a Miss Bolivia in the United States.

For those that return to the Valle Alto, reintegration has its challenges. In Arbieto, a long-term migrant who worked for over two decades in Florida, is now the mayor. Don Diogenes runs the local government, tends his peach orchards, and manages his large home. And yet, most of his family has remained in the United States. He told me that he owed it to his community to return.

In the global age we live in, where rural people travel vast distances in search of higher wages, many places like the Valle Alto exist. Some migrants leave never to return, yet most do return in some form or another, either for short visits or virtually through the flow of resources. Navigating lives across borders is not easy, but no matter where the residents of the Valle Alto travel, in their hearts there is still one place called home.
